Reversal of Roe v Wade raises some fundamental questions

It was five decades ago that Norma McCorvey, more popularly known by her pseudonym Jane Roe, first took to stand in court against the then-District Attorney of Dallas County, Henry Wade, and challenged the criminal abortion laws in Texas. The practice, considered unconstitutional by the state except when the continuation of pregnancy to term posed a significant danger to the life of the mother, Roe only succeeded in legalising abortion in the U.S, in 1973, something that would come to be known as the Supreme court's landmark ruling.

Roe v. Wade was significant in the sense that the case accorded women the freedom of choice and the right to bodily autonomy - guaranteeing the right to abortion in the first trimester, some regulation as to such of the government in the second trimester, and a pretext of danger to life to enforce the right to abortion in the third trimester. More so, it was a noteworthy ruling that set the precedent for subsequent cases of abortion and stimulated a new interpretation of the law that had been concluded from Roe v. Wade, 1973.

Fast forward fifty years later- and we find ourselves facing a most unique predicament. The 21st century may well be the hearth where the fires of revolutionary technological, developmental and socio-economic changes are being tended, but the dissolution of abortion rights and the reversal of Roe v.Wade might just be a cold draft of air that threatens to extinguish our progress and set us back by five decades.

Perhaps the premature leakage of a draft of the majority opinion of the Supreme court in May considering criminalising abortion rights may have made the reversal slightly less surprising and only imminent. But that is not to say the actual ruling enforced on the 24th of June, 2022 made the public any less wanting of answers to questions raised regarding the validity and integrity of fundamental rights, that of lawmaking itself as well as the place of politics and religion in the legal system.

But to facilitate debate to the largest extent of these issues and more, it's necessary to turn the pages of history and take note of the factors facilitating the legalisation of abortion in 1973.

The legal reasoning behind legalising abortion in 1973:

Today, the press and media have come to widely perpetuate the idea of the American Bill of Rights across global borders. Diligent fans of court dramas and criminal thrillers are sure to know of it. But it is buried subtly under this Bill that seeks to extend the functionality of the American constitution by according civil rights and liberties to the citizens, that we have the ninth amendment- the seed to the ultimate outcome of the legalisation of abortion.

The ninth amendment facilitated validity to the previous rights enumerated under the Bill and accredited their existence. But in doing so it also pointedly remarked that the Bill wasn't an exhaustive list, thereby claiming the existence of unenumerated rights- rights not explicitly mentioned within the Bill that couldn't be disparaged merely on the lack of their inclusion.

The right to privacy soon came to be acknowledged as one of these unenumerated rights and was subsequently deemed broad enough to be held applicable to the ruling of Roe v Wade- in the sense that the right to terminate an unwanted pregnancy came to be seen as a private one, thus legalising abortion.

The Supreme court claimed that this right could also be found in the fourteenth amendment whose liberty clause deemed abortion as but a private choice and enforcement of liberal autonomy

A bit of a stretch? Perhaps too broad of an interpretation? That's what two of the justices opposing the legalisation in the original Roe v Wade ruling of 7:2 thought so. And that's also what the overruling of Roe v Wade banked on-a too literal comprehension of the provisions of the constitution and the bill of rights. The Supreme Court claimed that there were no explicit mentions of "abortion rights" in either of these compilations and therefore based on the methodology of originalism, neither could abortion be acknowledged by the liberty clause, nor could it be legalised on the pretext of the right to privacy.

A motion that was met with significant outrage and dissent with thousands taking to the streets to clamour for the right to bodily autonomy and abortion to be seen as a human right, one can't but be apprehensive of the validity and credibility of one's "fundamental" rights. For just as swiftly and heartlessly that the overruling of Roe v Wade triggered other states into action aligning to the Supreme court's abortion ban, who knows, such a "warm" welcome might be extended to the rest of one's rights!

Law and Morality What this means is that the process of law-making must be questioned. The law and the legal system are not just what is but what should be, inducing a need for morality to the hard and fast rules that are enforced by the system.
In terms of Roe v Wade, in particular, the reversal of the original ruling was based entirely on a different stance regarding the interpretation and application of existing amendments. Here the legal system and the law were simply what is rather than what they should be. The latter would essentially mean recognizing the need to protect abortion rights and guard them as constitutional since abortion becomes the direct manifestation of the right to bodily autonomy so long as the viability of the foetus is considered.

Are there not wider implications of the laws themselves that must be evaluated before initiating sweeping changes on the need to rectify an academic discrepancy? Surely it would only take a simple cost-benefit analysis to determine that the advantages of abortion rights greatly do or could come to with greater policy specifications- outweigh the detriments?

Law should be receptive to the implications of continuing with an unwanted pregnancy- an obligation that may be thrust upon women by the manner of pure violence. It should recognize the psychological distress induced as a result of unwanted pregnancies, specifically in the case of teenage pregnancies- where one lacks the intellectual maturity to deal with the circumstances and is subject to social and physical challenges. It should recognize the rights that women hold over their bodies and by extension life and that they do hold constitutional significance.

Criminalising abortions would not guarantee that these would cease in numbers, only that the women still needing the practice would be forced to seek out fewer safe options.

We must constantly strive to be the best that we can, be till the world reflects our collective ideals and this means embedding morality in law and thinking about what should be. It is as a consequence of this that the law should be allowed to be enforced and amended.

The place of politics and religion in the legal system - The slogan “Separation of the church and state” could be clearly heard amongst the protesters following the Supreme court's ruling. And the reasoning behind the same is as such- the majority of the American population is largely Christian. And let's just say that religion doesn't exactly look upon abortion empathetically or something that should be encouraged.

The undeniable connection of politics with the law thus puts one in a bit of a predicament when considering this Christian majority extend their democratic rights by voting for someone who represents their ideals i.e. someone who is Christian. While this is just the rightful exercise of the democratic rights of the citizens, what does get highlighted is the need of separating the church from the state. For one must consider the role religion and religious people play as part of the vote bank in influencing the decisions of the state. Who knows, the American decision might even permeate throughout the global scene and convince other conservative Christian countries to ban abortion.

The law does not protect, punish, or guarantee rights exclusively to a particular religious or social group within any nation. It should not alter its form to perpetuate the opinions or beliefs of any particular social group, for one may be a majority or a minority, deeply religious or an atheist (with obvious diverse specifications) but in the end, the law affects us and responds to us in the same manner and is thus inherently equalising. This thus indicates the need to separate the church and the state, in order for the law to exist rightfully and treat all of its citizens in an equal manner.

The Reversal of Roe v Wade has definitely given us all a lot to think about. It is pertinent to consider what this could mean not just for the validity of the rest of the fundamental rights, but also for the methodology of law making and our social interactions.
