Domestic Violence Against Women: Recognizing early signs which would help prevent abuse

Domestic violence is quickly traversing across national boundaries as well as socio-economic, cultural, racial, and class distinctions. Its incidence is also extensive, making it seem an accepted behavior. Domestic violence has serious impacts on women's health and well-being and its continued existence is morally indefensible. Yet this major problem has been so widely ignored and so little understood.

Domestic violence is not always as obvious as you might think. That's because domestic abuse is about controlling someone's mind and emotions as much as hurting their body. It can be hard for you to see your partner's actions for what they really are. Usually, physical abuse isn't what comes first. The abuse creeps up slowly through verbal harassment. A putdown here or there. An odd excuse to keep you away from family or friends, or make you ask for an OK to see friends and family. Controlling your dressing sense, while keeping a tab on every person you interact with, or criticizing you for meeting people you are comfortable with. Sometimes, abusers embarrass you in front of people you love and respect, implicitly forcing you to avoid people in general. This technique often makes you feel less worthy of yourself and vulnerable, after which the violence often ramps up since you've been cut off from other people. By then, you feel trapped.

Often, abusers attempt to control your money, putting you on an "allowance" and ensuring every dollar you spend is with their consent. This essentially makes you dependent on your partner for basic necessities like food and clothing and even getting medical care. An attempt of "disobedience" on your side leads to yelling, throwing punches at walls, and making you feel smaller to them.

Every woman is still not aware of sexual abuse. CONSENT is essential. If a man forces you to have sex, despite your resistance, it is considered abuse, no matter what your relationship status is. Forcing you to dress in a sexual way and making you feel obliged to have sex with them are all forms of domestic abuse.

If you're afraid of your partner, that's a big red flag. You may be scared to say what you think, to bring up certain topics, or say no to sex. No matter the reason, fear has no place in a healthy relationship.

If you feel like you're being abused, there's a good chance you may be, and it's worth getting help.
