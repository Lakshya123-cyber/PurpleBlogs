The Nineteenth Century's Pride and Prejudice: An insight into Women's Rights

The very first time I watched the famous film Pride and Prejudice four years ago, I assumed the storyline to only revolve around an anonymous figure of a rich, single man. However, this misconception was dispelled once I thoroughly read the novel a few months back, only to realise it is concerned with the plight of a poor, single woman. For the past three centuries, women's rights have been a hotly debated and significant issue. Pride and Prejudice, a work of literary fiction by Jane Austen, is well-known and highly regarded for its controversial yet comprehensive ideas and viewpoints on female liberties and feminist roles.

It could be challenging for contemporary readers to comprehend the significance of marriage in the lives of Elizabeth Bennet and her sisters, who are characters in the book. Today's young women have a wide range of alternatives available to them when it comes to their future: they can marry, of course, but they can also attend college, pursue any job route that may interest them, and live independently of family members or chaperones. Young women in Austen's time did not have access to these benefits. The girls of the middle and upper classes were allowed to attend school, but their education there focused more on accomplishment than it did on broadening their academic knowledge.

"Compared with some families, I believe we were; but such of us as wished to learn never wanted the means. We were always encouraged to read and had all the masters that were necessary. Those who chose to be idle, certainly might." This was a statement quoted by Elizabeth Bennet in the novel. A bright, inquisitive young lady like Elizabeth Bennet would have naturally been able to extend her knowledge on her own through reading. She describes her and her sisters' education as being accessible but unstructured, where Fitzwilliam Darcy, her lover, concomitantly points out that a truly admirable woman will develop her mind through substantial reading.

Apart from educational differences, the social and economic expectations of women in nineteenth-century England were illustrated through Jane Austen's depictions of various perspectives on distinct attitudes and expectations concerning love and marriage. Even though the novel featured a variety of characters and viewpoints, Austen's female protagonists were frequently encouraged to focus on and strive for marriage. Women's lives were meaningless and could only be completed by marrying upper-class men and achieving financial security. The relationship between the characters Charlotte and Mr. Collins is one of the first ones in the novel that fits into this category. While rejecting any romantic impulses, Charlotte marries Mr. Collins exclusively out of mercenary self-interest. Jane Austen also depicted this in a line from her book, "solely from the pure and disinterested desire of an establishment", marking marriage as the "pleasantest preservative from want".

Even though there were many issues faced regarding love and marriage in the 19th century, Austen brings out themes of feminism and empowerment of women with the beautiful portrayal of Elizabeth's character. Elizabeth rejects Darcy's initial proposal to her because it lacks heart and sincerity and seems like he is just settling with her. She understands that by doing so, she would be placing herself in a marriage that would resemble that of her parents, who would be unhappy and lacking in mutual respect. This concept put her happiness first rather than her wealth and social standing, which was the antithesis of the prevailing ideas at the time!

A girl or young woman today is discouraged or denied when she expresses a desire to do something or want anything by being told that she is "too young", "too old", or "she doesn't know anything!"

When you look at Elizabeth in her past, you see a young woman who was subject to even stricter limitations. Yet she exuded confidence. She was witty and intelligent. A beautiful girl distinguished by her wit and lighthearted humour. A young lady who acknowledged her mistakes. Being flawless is a fantasy. Jane Austen had depicted her as a modern lady who aspires to be a strong independent female. A woman who was capable of taking care of herself despite being conscious of her flaws.

After much research and reading this book, I'd like to draw a conclusion that ties into the concept of contemporary feminism and women's rights: Young girls and women who can speak up and who aren't meant to be ideal but rather real are what we need. A flawed but reasonable, logical, and morally upright lady. A woman who is always eager for improvement.

The fundamental message of Pride and Prejudice by Jane Austen is that marriages should be based on physical attraction and love rather than ensuring a husband's financial security or having women raise the heir to his riches or legacy. She expresses her political views and wants her readers to understand the lack of equality women were receiving. Jane Austen uses the depiction of the Bennet siblings and their various viewpoints on romance and marriage to highlight the social and economic inequality of women in nineteenth-century England.
